The most frustrating part of running a business is constantly having to keep track of ever-changing dates. Missing a return means needless penalties and late-payment interest. In this article I explain the logic of the tax calendar — so that **you don’t have to memorise the dates.**

## Which returns does an SME usually file?

Although it varies by the type of business, most companies have these on their agenda:

- **VAT return** — usually in monthly periods
- **Withholding and premium service return** — a filing that combines withholding and social-security notifications
- **Provisional (corporate/income) tax return** — in three-month periods
- **Annual income or corporate tax return** — once a year, at the close of the accounting period

Alongside these, additional filings such as stamp duty and the Ba-Bs forms may also come up.

## Why aren’t the dates “fixed”?

The official deadlines shift to the next business day when they fall on a weekend or a public holiday. From time to time the administration also grants extensions. That’s why memorising “the such-and-such day of the month” can be misleading. **Drawing up the current, personalised calendar is my job** — I remind you period by period.

## What happens if you’re late?

A return not filed on time can result in an irregularity penalty, a tax-loss penalty and late-payment interest. Even a small delay can turn into a serious cost when it recurs. The good news: with regular follow-up, almost all of this can be prevented.

## How to make tracking easier

1. **Send your documents in a regular flow** — don’t pile them up and leave them to the last day.
2. Use **digital storage**; e-documents already sit in the system.
3. **Leave the date-tracking to me** — I remind, prepare and file on time.
