The company made a profit, there’s cash in the till, and the partner wants to take that money home. The first way that comes to mind is usually a dividend distribution — and it’s often the most expensive one. There’s a smarter way to move the same money into the partner’s pocket that also lowers the company’s tax: the **director’s fee (huzur hakkı).** Below I’ve compared the two in plain language.

## First, don’t withdraw cash without documentation

The mistake I see most is the partner occasionally taking cash out of the till and issuing no document in return. This inflates the “receivables from partners” line; it requires notional interest (adat) to be calculated on it, creates a hidden-profit risk, and makes your balance sheet look weak to the bank. If money is going to leave the company, it should leave through a **formal, documented** channel.

## Why is a dividend expensive?

With a dividend, the money is taxed twice: first corporate tax on the company’s earnings, then withholding on distribution. When these two layers stack, the amount that reaches the partner drops noticeably.

## Why is a director’s fee cheaper?

A director’s fee is the amount paid to a partner who serves in the company’s management, in return for that role, and it’s legally treated as **a wage.** This distinction changes everything:

- **It’s recorded as an expense on the company side.** The director’s fee paid reduces the corporate-tax base — that is, unlike a dividend, it lowers the company’s tax.
- **There’s an exemption on the individual side.** Because it’s a wage, it benefits from the minimum-wage exemption. In 2026, the portion corresponding to the monthly minimum wage (gross TRY 33,030) is exempt from both income and stamp tax; the portion above this is taxed normally. The condition: not receiving a wage from another employer that month.
- **No extra social-security premium is deducted from a Bağ-Kur-insured partner.**

As a result, across a wide range of amounts, taking money out via a director’s fee leaves a noticeable difference in the partner’s pocket compared with a dividend.

## But it isn’t unlimited — watch three points

A director’s fee isn’t a magic exemption but a sloping ramp:

1. **The cumulative tariff.** The income-tax base accumulates over the year. The partner who’s in a low bracket in January climbs into higher brackets as the payment grows. At very high amounts the advantage erodes.
2. **Arm’s-length conformity.** The payment must be in return for a genuine role. Paying a high director’s fee to a partner who doesn’t actually work is treated as a hidden profit; the expense is rejected and a penalty is added.
3. **Formalise the decision.** The director’s fee must be tied to a partners’/general assembly resolution; the amount and period must be in writing.

## The right approach: plan at the start of the year

The costliest mistake I see in the field is paying the director’s fee randomly throughout the year and not tracking the cumulative base. The partner runs into an unexpected tax at year-end from a payment they thought was “tax-free.” The right method: set the target net amount at the start of the year, plan the monthly director’s fee against the exemption and the first-bracket limit, and leave the rest to a dividend. This planning doesn’t work retroactively — it’s laid out at the **start of the year**, not in December.

For most SMEs, the smartest option is a **hybrid structure** that keeps the director’s fee at a reasonable, arm’s-length level and leaves the rest to a dividend.
